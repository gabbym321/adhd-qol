What is ADHD?

ADHD stands for Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder. It is a condition with brain development that affects attention, the ability to sit still, and self control.

How Does ADHD Affect Adolescents?

Symptoms of ADHD are broken up into three groups. These groups and Symptoms are known as:

Inattenation:

  • Procrastination or the failure to complete tasks.
  • Disorganization
  • Lack of focus and getting distracted my thing that may seem insignificant to others.
  • Being forgetful. Those with ADHD may forget to turn in an assignment or go to an important doctors appointment.
  • Not listening to others, inability to stay on one topic, not following social rules.

Hyperactivity:

  • Excessive movement and fidgeting in seat.
  • Inability to stay seated for long periods of time.
  • Restlessness
  • Excessive talking

Impulsivity:

  • Impatience
  • Difficulty waiting their turn which often results in frequent interruptions and blurting out answers
  • Speaking at inappropriate times
  • Doing risky things without thinking about consequences


Here is a few systems public schools have in place to help



An Individualized Education Program (IEP) Is a legal document that lays out a special education plan in order to accommodate a child's exact needs. One studentâ€™s IEP could look totally different than the nexts. All public schools have this program in place; this is covered by the Special Education Law. Obtaining an IEP is a large process. This begins with an evaluation to check if the student qualifies, while also measuring their strengths and weaknesses. An IEP can stay with a student up until college; with yearly meetings, reports and evaluation checking up on the progress and making any adjustments needed to the IEP.




504 plans are blueprints that public schools make in order to insure that students with disabilities are getting the same rights as others. This is covered under section 504 of Rehabilitation act, which is a civil rights law. What a 504 plan does is give students special accommodations such as extended time on tests or smaller class sizes. There is many ways to qualify for a 504 plan. Often schools look at multiple sources. For example they might do a medical diagnosis, check grades and ask for teacher recommendations. Unlike an IEP, a full evaluation is not required.


My Plan

Here is what I would like do do in order to help students with ADHD:


Although there are systems in public schools that help students with ADHD and other disabilities, these programs are often difficult to get into and work more on accommodating the needs of the student rather than helping them with skill work.

What I plan on creating is a class where students with ADHD or who inhibit symptoms of sort can go to and work on skills that they may have a hard time with due to these symptoms. Some skill work may include: organization, attentiveness, techniques to deal with impulsivity, and more.

Students who are not apart of the class but who excel academically can come in to speak with the class about techniques they found that helps them and struggles they also face in school.

Lastly, studies have shown that 64% of indivisuals with ADHD have another disorder such as depression, anxiety, oppositional defiant disorder, and more. It has been proven that psychotherapy is an effective way of dealing with these disorders; therefore, I plan to have guidance counselors come in weekly and do both private and group psychotherapy.
